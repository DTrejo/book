If I Ever Look for Another Software Engineering Job, Here's How I'd Go about It

0) Spend at least a week studying data structures and algorithms. Yes, I have a Github, but if you want to get into college, you should probably be prepared for the SAT. Also, make sure all my resume bullet points speak to business outcomes. Not sure what I mean? Go read some http://kalzumeus.com, especially "don't call yourself a programmer."

1) Ask all friends for companies they think they'd want to work for. The least insane companies are probably bootstrapped, B2B, growing, making money, and employ 50+ employees. And of course it's a good idea if they use my preferred tech stack, otherwise I'm going to be frustrated because Ruby is so slow.

2) Set up informational interviews at cafés with all the hiring managers, and ask my company red flags and qualification questions which range from what VCs want to know all the way to what engineers want to know. I'd also evaluate their current marketing and sales efforts, because weakness in these areas spells doom, no matter the skill of their engineering team. TODO: how to evaluate sales/marketing ability.

3) Take notes during or after each interview. Most importantly on the project and team they'd expect me to work on upon joining. Especially on the logistics: # interviews; average weeks until hiring decision is made; is the salary range actually what's been posted; double-checking the position you'd interview for is what you thought it was.

4) After all informational interviews have finished, promote companies I liked to the next stage in my pipeline, and schedule all first round interviews for one week.

4.5) Get yourself on Hired, and set your salary to 1.5x to start. if you're getting offers before noon, crank it to 1.75x. If not, lower it to 1.25x. for the rest of the day. If you got no offers in the afternoon, lower it to 0.10x for the rest of the week. Ruthlessly reject companies who don't fit your tech stack, or company size requirements. They'll just waste your time and use your mental energy. You can use these offers in step 6! It typically takes ~1 week to get accepted into a Hired round, unless you reach out to someone who works there and ask to be expedited :)

5) Stave off deciding on an offer "out of respect for the CEOs whose interview processes are not yet finished."

5.5) when I'm given an offer, ask "is this the best you can give me?" to get the recruiter to bump the offer up to the highest their able to, without needing approval from their boss.
